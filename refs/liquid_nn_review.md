# Liquid Neural Networks and Quantum Approaches for ODE/PDE Forecasting

Liquid neural networks (LNNs) are a family of continuous-time recurrent models whose hidden state is governed by a learned ordinary differential equation with input-dependent, time-varying dynamics. Because the state evolves through a differential equation rather than a fixed discrete recurrence, the family is a natural fit for forecasting problems defined by ODEs and PDEs, where the target is itself a continuous dynamical system. The "quantum liquid neural network" is not yet an established, named architecture — a full-text search of arXiv and OpenAlex returns no paper using that exact term, so the quantum half of this review is assembled from its constituent literatures: quantum machine learning foundations, quantum reservoir computing (the quantum analogue of the liquid-state machine), and the fast-moving line of quantum and hybrid quantum-classical neural networks that solve differential equations. This framing lets you position a quantum-liquid model as a genuine contribution rather than an incremental relabelling.

## Liquid neural networks

### Foundations: continuous-time neural computation

The modern continuous-time thread begins with neural ordinary differential equations, which replace discrete layers with a parameterized derivative of the hidden state. The original work parameterizes "the derivative of the hidden state using a neural network," with outputs "computed using a black-box differential equation solver" ([Chen et al. 2018](https://arxiv.org/abs/1806.07366)); this is the substrate on which the liquid models build. The liquid-time-constant idea was first formalized as a subclass of continuous-time RNNs "with varying neuronal time-constant realized by their nonlinear synaptic transmission model," inspired by "the communication principles in the nervous system of small species," and shown to be a universal approximator of finite trajectories of continuous dynamical systems ([Hasani et al. 2018](https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.00321)); this theoretical result that directly motivates their use for trajectory forecasting.

### Core Liquid Neural Network models

The canonical LTC paper introduces "a new class of time-continuous recurrent neural network models" built from "networks of linear first-order dynamical systems modulated via nonlinear interlinked gates," yielding "dynamical systems with varying (i.e., liquid) time-constants coupled to their hidden state, with outputs being computed by numerical differential equation solvers" ([Hasani et al. 2021](https://doi.org/10.1609/aaai.v35i9.16936)). The same group's neural circuit policies work demonstrated that extremely compact wired networks — on the order of tens of neurons, can control a vehicle end-to-end with interpretable, "auditable" dynamics ([Lechner et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42256-020-00237-3)), establishing the parameter-efficiency argument that recurs throughout the LNN literature.

The principal bottleneck of ODE-based models is the numerical solver in the loop. Closed-form continuous-time (CfC) networks address this directly: the authors note that the expressive power of continuous-time models "is bottlenecked by numerical DE solvers" and show it is possible to "closely approximate" the solver's interaction analytically, removing it from the forward pass ([Hasani et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42256-022-00556-7)). For forecasting on irregular grids, the companion ODE-LSTM handles the "long-term dependencies in irregularly-sampled time series" that standard RNNs mishandle ([Lechner & Hasani 2020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.04418)). The robustness case was made most visibly in drone flight, where liquid networks gave "robust flight navigation out of distribution," transferring across seasons and environments not seen in training ([Chahine et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1126/scirobotics.adc8892)).

### Scaling, hardware, and architectural variants

Bringing liquid dynamics to long-sequence modeling motivated a merger with structured state-space models: Liquid-S4 shows that an S4 model improves "when the structural SSM ... is given by a linear liquid time-constant (LTC) state-space model," exploiting an "input-dependent state transition module" ([Hasani et al. 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.12951)). Scaling the non-linear variants is the target of LrcSSM, "a non-linear recurrent model that processes long sequences as fast as today's linear state-space layers" by forcing a diagonal Jacobian, and, relevant for training stability on stiff dynamics, it "offers a formal gradient-stability guarantee that other input-varying systems such as Liquid-S4 and Mamba do not provide" ([Farsang et al. 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.21717)). Connectivity structure also matters for control robustness: parameterizing recurrent connectivity as low-rank and sparse improves closed-loop generalization ([Tumma et al. 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.03915)). On the deployment side, LNNs have been mapped onto neuromorphic silicon, with a Loihi-2 implementation reporting strong efficiency on image classification ([Pawlak et al. 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.20590)).

### Forecasting and time-series applications

The forecasting evidence for LNNs is accumulating across domains. In telecom, a "first principles" treatment argues LNNs counter the "substantial performance degradation in dynamic environments with varying data distributions" that afflicts standard models ([Zhu et al. 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.02352)). For financial time series, LNNs have been applied to short-horizon Henry Hub natural-gas spot-price forecasting, chosen because they "adapt continuously" to volatile, regime-switching dynamics that limit traditional models ([Liu et al. 2026](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24788)). A head-to-head clinical and sequential benchmarking study frames the core distinction cleanly, noting that discrete RNNs and LSTMs "often failing to capture the fluid temporal dynamics of real-world physical processes," which CfC networks address by "modeling the hidden state evolution as a continuous differential equation" ([Thu et al. 2026](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.27467)). The liquid mechanism is also being absorbed into other backbones. A multi-timescale "Liquid-Mamba" injects "input-conditioned multi-timescale liquid discretization" into a selective SSM ([Gao et al. 2026](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22801)), and it is used as an interpretable latent dynamics model for turbofan degradation on the C-MAPSS prognostics benchmark ([Nie et al. 2026](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01986)).

## Quantum approaches

### Quantum foundations: QML and quantum reservoirs

The quantum side rests on two pillars. The first is the general promise, and known caveats, of quantum machine learning, surveyed in the field's most-cited reference ([Biamonte et al. 2017](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature23474)). The second is directly analogous to the "liquid" idea: quantum reservoir computing uses the natural, disordered dynamics of a quantum system as the untrained recurrent substrate, "harnessing disordered-ensemble quantum dynamics for machine learning" ([Fujii & Nakajima 2017](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevApplied.8.024030)). Because the classical liquid-state machine is a reservoir model, quantum reservoir computing is the closest existing analogue to a "quantum liquid" network and is the natural point of contact for a quantum-liquid contribution.

### Quantum and hybrid neural networks for differential equations

The most active quantum thread for your problem set is quantum physics-informed neural networks (QPINNs) and related solvers. An early demonstration designed "a simple Quantum PINN to solve the one-dimensional Poisson equation" on a quantum processing unit ([Markidis 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.14754)). A self-adaptive variant generalized a Chebyshev-polynomial QML framework to the 2D Poisson equation, second-order linear equations, and nonlinear Duffing and Riccati equations ([Setty et al. 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.09215)). QPINNs were then extended from ODEs to multi-variable PDEs such as the Poisson and heat equations ([Panichi et al. 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.12244)), and to nonlocal problems (integro-differential and fractional integro-PDEs) via "an affine feature map and variational quantum circuits" ([Gupta et al. 2026](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26865)).

Two 2026 hybrid works are worth watching because they report the kind of head-to-head comparison a referee will expect. One develops "hybrid networks consisting of quantum circuits combined with classical layers" and tests systematically whether quantum computing improves a PINN's ability to solve PDEs — a direct benchmark against classical PINNs ([Klement et al. 2026](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.15046)). The other builds a Hybrid Quantum-Classical CNN multigrid solver that maps stencil operators onto quantum convolutional circuits ([Zhai et al. 2026](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24196)). Most foundational for a "quantum liquid" framing, however, is the unified continuous-time formalism of Quantum Neural ODEs and PDEs, which extends "the continuous-time formalism of classical neural ordinary and partial differential equations into quantum machine learning and quantum control," governed by "generalised Schrödinger-type Hamiltonian dynamics" with adjoint-based gradients ([Cao et al. 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.18326)). This paper is the strongest existing bridge between the neural-ODE lineage that underlies LNNs and a genuinely quantum dynamical model, and is the reference against which a quantum-liquid architecture should be positioned.

## What is established, what is open

The LNN side is mature enough to cite with confidence: the LTC/CfC/NCP line is peer-reviewed (AAAI, two *Nature Machine Intelligence* papers, one *Science Robotics* paper); the universal-approximation and gradient-stability results are formal, and forecasting applications now span telecom, energy, clinical, and prognostics data. The quantum side is earlier-stage and preprint-dominated. QPINNs demonstrably solve textbook ODEs and low-dimensional PDEs, and the Quantum Neural ODE/PDE formalism supplies the continuous-time scaffolding, but there is no published architecture that fuses input-dependent liquid time-constants with a quantum substrate. That absence is the gap your paper occupies, and the honest way to frame the contribution is as the first coupling of the liquid-time-constant mechanism to a quantum dynamical model for ODE/PDE forecasting, benchmarked against both classical LNNs and existing QPINNs.
